Introduction

From the sunlit surface to the pitch-black abyss, oceans hold endless marvels that fuel our curiosity and inspire exploration. They generate over half of the oxygen we breathe, house some of the oldest living creatures, and contain ecosystems more diverse than any on land.

Did you know?

The ocean covers more than 70% of our planet’s surface? It holds about 96.5% of all Earth's water! yet we’ve explored only a fraction of its vast depths. Beneath its shimmering waves lies a mysterious world teeming with incredible creatures, breathtaking landscapes, and fascinating secrets. From glowing deep-sea organisms to underwater mountain ranges taller than Everest.

Plastic Pollution

Plastic pollution has become one of the greatest threats facing our oceans today. Every year, millions of tons of plastic waste find their way into the sea, harming marine life and disrupting delicate ecosystems. From tiny microplastics to massive floating debris, plastic pollution affects creatures large and small — whales, turtles, fish, and seabirds often mistake plastic for food, leading to injury or death.

Plastic in Deepest Areas

The Mariana Trench—the deepest point in the ocean—extends nearly 10,975 meters (36,000 feet) down in a remote part of the Pacific Ocean. A study revealed that a plastic bag, like the kind given away at grocery stores, is now the deepest known piece of plastic trash, inside the Mariana Trench.

Rising Sea Levels

As global temperatures continue to rise, polar ice caps and glaciers are melting at an alarming rate, leading to rapidly rising sea levels. This not only threatens low-lying coastal communities with flooding and erosion, but also disrupts delicate marine ecosystems. Saltwater intrusion into freshwater habitats, the loss of wetlands, and the submersion of vital breeding grounds all pose significant risks to both marine life and human populations.

Multiple Ocean Species Extinction

Climate change is pushing many ocean species to the brink of extinction. Rising sea temperatures, ocean acidification, and deoxygenation are altering habitats faster than marine life can adapt. Coral reefs—often referred to as the rainforests of the sea—are bleaching and dying at unprecedented rates, leaving countless fish, invertebrates, and marine mammals without shelter or food. From tiny plankton to large predators, the entire food chain is being affected.
